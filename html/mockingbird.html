To kill a Mockingbird

A child’s-eye view of racial injustice in the American South. Through Scout’s
eyes we watch her father, the principled lawyer Atticus Finch, courageously
defend Tom Robinson, a Black man falsely accused of raping a white woman,
and in doing so expose the town’s deep-seated racism and moral failures. The
trial’s outcome and its tragic consequences force Scout and her brother Jem to
confront the loss of innocence, while the reclusive neighbor Arthur “Boo”
Radley—once the subject of childhood fears and rumors—ultimately reveals
unexpected kindness by protecting the children, teaching them empathy and
the importance of seeing people for who they really are.

Title: To kill a Mockingbird

Author: Nelle Harper Lee (commonly credited as Harper Lee)

First published: July 11, 1960 (United States)

Original publisher: J. B. Lippincott & Co.

Format & length: Originally published as a hardcover novel; typical modern paperback editions run ~280–320 pages (commonly cited as 281 pages).

Genre: Southern Gothic; Bildungsroman (coming-of-age); Social novel addressing racial injustice.

Narrator / Point of view: First-person retrospective narration by Jean Louise “Scout” Finch (an adult narrator recalling childhood events).

Setting: Maycomb, Alabama — a fictional, small Southern town during the 1930s (Great Depression era).
